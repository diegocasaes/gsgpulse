The Strait of Hormuz Is Closed — And the Global South Is Paying the Price

Since February 28, 2026, the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil and gas passes — has been effectively closed following the US–Israel air campaign against Iran and the assassination of Supreme Leader Khamenei. Iran’s Revolutionary Guard Corps responded with a maritime blockade, mining operations, and attacks on merchant shipping. The US counter-blockade of Iranian ports, launched April 13, and the subsequent Operation Project Freedom (a US Navy merchant escort mission) escalated the crisis further before Trump paused the operation on May 6, citing “great progress” toward an agreement. That agreement has not materialized. The closure is now entering its 13th week.

The consequences for the Global South are severe and asymmetric. Nearly 84% of Gulf crude and 83% of LNG transiting the Strait was destined for Asia — roughly 70% to China, India, Japan, and South Korea. Pakistan and Bangladesh are among the hardest-hit economies, facing fuel shortages, inflation spikes, and GDP contraction. The crisis has given fresh urgency to the debate within BRICS about energy supply diversification and has handed India — as BRICS chair hosting the September summit — an unusually complex diplomatic opening: to broker between its strategic autonomy, its energy dependency, and its aspirations to lead Global South climate finance architecture simultaneously. The Iran crisis is not a sideshow to Global South geopolitics in 2026 — it is the central forcing function.

DRC · Congo High
Mineral Sovereignty Offensive: Cobalt Quotas, Audits, and 90 Revoked Mining Titles
President Tshisekedi’s April 25 cabinet directive mandated a comprehensive audit of all mining exports, targeting revenue leakage. CAMI (Mining Cadastre) revoked 90 titles covering 12,767 km² across five provinces. Cobalt export quotas for 2026–27 are set at 96,600 MT annually — less than half 2024 output — creating ripple effects across Asian EV battery supply chains.
Why it matters

This is the most assertive mineral governance move by any African state in decades. It establishes a precedent: resource states can unilaterally restructure supply chains that Western and Chinese EV manufacturers had assumed were secured. If enforcement holds, it could template Zambia, Zimbabwe, and Namibia. If it collapses under capital flight, it will entrench the argument that sovereignty is performative without institutional capacity to back it.

Brazil High
Amazon at Historic Low — But 2026 Elections Put It Back at Risk
Deforestation in the Legal Amazon dropped to its lowest level since 2014, confirming a 50% reduction since Lula took office. The government committed R$3.1 billion ($617.5M) to the Eco Invest program on May 25, targeting bioeconomy, sustainable tourism, and Amazon infrastructure. Lula’s same-day visit to Manaus was simultaneously ecological and electoral — positioning Omar Aziz for the Amazonas governorship with Amazon stewardship as the political brand.
Why it matters

Brazil’s Amazon trajectory is one of the most consequential climate governance stories on the planet. The numbers are real and the trend is meaningful. But current polls show Lula in a tight race, and a more agriculture-friendly Congress is possible. The Eco Invest announcement signals that the Lula government understands Amazon success as a political asset to be actively managed, not merely a policy outcome. The risk is that this becomes campaign material before it becomes durable institutional architecture.

Bangladesh High
Hormuz Worst-Hit: GDP Contraction, Fuel Crisis, and a Climate Debt Trap
Bangladesh has been identified as one of the most severely impacted Global South economies from the Strait of Hormuz fuel crisis, with recession-like GDP conditions projected. The country simultaneously faces climate-intensified cyclones, rising sea levels, and a pre-existing debt burden. Bangladesh contributed negligibly to global emissions yet is absorbing consequences of decisions made in Washington, Tehran, and Tel Aviv.
Why it matters

Bangladesh is a paradigm case for the cruel intersection of multiple, mutually reinforcing crises: climate vulnerability, energy import dependency, and limited fiscal space. This is the political argument for climate reparations and loss & damage in its most concrete form — a country that did not cause the problem, holds no leverage in the geopolitical dynamics producing it, and is paying the price.

Sub-Saharan Africa High
Africa’s Debt Crisis Tightens the Climate Finance Trap
22 low-income African countries are in or at high risk of debt distress. Debt service has risen to 43% of budget revenue — up from 38% in 2023. Net capital flows to EMDEs have turned negative. The sovereign-bank nexus is growing fastest in Sub-Saharan Africa, where government debt problems can trigger banking crises.
Why it matters

Countries paying 43% of revenue on debt service cannot simultaneously invest in adaptation infrastructure, transition off fossil fuels, and maintain social spending. The irony is structural: Africa holds 30% of the world’s critical minerals — the raw material inputs for the very energy transition it cannot afford to finance for itself.

Global — Indigenous Peoples High
Less Than 1% of Global Climate Finance Reaches Indigenous Peoples — UNPFII Escalates
Between 2011 and 2020, Indigenous peoples and local communities received less than 1% of global climate finance despite managing at least 25% of carbon-capturing land globally. At UNPFII, advocates cited devastation from flooding and wildfires and called for direct access mechanisms. OHCHR opened a formal call for inputs on land demarcation and titling in 2026.
Why it matters

The 1% figure is not just morally indefensible — it is strategically irrational. Indigenous-managed territories consistently show better biodiversity and forest outcomes than state or corporate-managed equivalents. The OHCHR titling call for inputs may be the procedural step toward binding frameworks worth tracking for COP31 and the CBD.
