The DRC Minerals-for-Peace Bargain Is Fracturing — Constitutional Challenge, Ceasefire Violations, and a Template Under Fire

The US-brokered December 2025 minerals-for-peace deal linking DRC-Rwanda relations and access to cobalt, copper, lithium, and manganese to American strategic interests is now facing a constitutional crisis inside the DRC. A coalition of Congolese lawyers and human rights defenders brought the agreement to the country's Constitutional Court in January 2026, arguing that an international agreement of this magnitude requires parliamentary ratification or a popular referendum — neither of which occurred. The constitutional challenge is active. Meanwhile, on the ground in eastern Congo, both M23 rebel forces and Congolese army units are accusing each other of ceasefire violations, with residents reporting clashes and rebel advances in multiple localities. The peace architecture that justified the minerals access deal is eroding from both ends simultaneously.

The structural stakes extend well beyond the DRC. The Trump administration built this deal as a proof-of-concept for a new model of US strategic engagement with the Global South: minerals access in exchange for security guarantees, sidelining traditional multilateral frameworks and conditionality. Scrutiny of the DRC deal is now seeding skepticism across the continent — Mongabay reports other African nations are signing on to similar arrangements even as the DRC template shows its cracks. This matters for the entire energy transition supply chain: the DRC alone holds an estimated 70% of global cobalt reserves. How sovereignty questions around this deal are resolved — in court, in parliament, or through continued conflict — will set precedents that shape how African nations negotiate the next decade of minerals diplomacy under multipolar great power pressure.

Why this story won: It is the only story in this scan that simultaneously touches minerals sovereignty, armed conflict, constitutional governance, US-Africa relations, critical energy transition supply chains, and the structural credibility of minerals-for-security dealmaking as a diplomatic model. Every major thematic thread in this edition — from Africa debt to just transition to Global South 2.0 — runs through it.

Brazil High Relevance
Agribusiness Caucus Strips Amazon Satellite Enforcement — Lula Forced to Choose
Brazil's Congress passed legislation on May 20, 2026, banning remote embargoes — the satellite-based tool allowing IBAMA to block commercial use of illegally deforested land. The bill would eliminate approximately 70% of IBAMA's Amazon enforcement capacity. IBAMA officials say banning the technology is equivalent to "going back to the fax machine" with only 1,250 agents to patrol a forest the size of Western Europe. The bill is part of a broader "Destruction Package" advancing in Congress ahead of October general elections.
Why It MattersBrazil achieved three consecutive years of falling deforestation under Lula — down 50% since 2022, the lowest since 2012. This bill, if signed, would gut the enforcement architecture behind that progress. Lula faces a structural bind: veto the agribusiness bloc and lose critical electoral support, or sign and betray the climate legacy he built at COP30 in Belém. The October election makes either choice costly. The BR-319 highway ($75M investment) compounds the signal.

DRC High Relevance
Minerals-for-Peace Deal Under Constitutional Attack as Eastern Congo Burns
The US-DRC-Rwanda strategic minerals agreement signed in December 2025 is being challenged at the DRC Constitutional Court by a coalition of lawyers and human rights defenders who argue the deal required parliamentary ratification. Simultaneously, the ceasefire in eastern DRC is fragmenting, with M23 forces and FARDC trading accusations of violations. Congolese communities near mining areas report fear of exploitation as the US secures access to cobalt, copper, lithium, and manganese assets on a shortlist provided to Washington in January 2026.
Why It MattersThe DRC holds ~70% of global cobalt — indispensable to the EV and battery storage industries underpinning the global energy transition. The constitutional challenge, if successful, could void the agreement and destabilize the nascent peace architecture. The model of resource-access-as-security-guarantee is being tested here first, and the outcome will determine whether other African nations resist or replicate it.

India / Pakistan High Relevance
Post-Sindoor Arms Race: Precision Strikes, Suspended Water Rights, Frozen Dialogue
One year after Operation Sindoor (May 7, 2025) — India's missile strike campaign following the Pahalgam attack — both nations are locked in an accelerating arms race. India's military expenditure rose 8.9% in 2025 to $92.1B; Pakistan's up 11% to $11.9B. Both are targeting 13–15% increases in 2026-27 budgets. The Indus Water Treaty remains suspended, creating food and energy security risk for Pakistan. India has adopted a posture of "strategic indifference" toward Pakistan as China becomes its primary security focus.
Why It MattersThe Indus Water Treaty suspension is not just a bilateral issue — it's a climate-water nexus ticking clock. Pakistan is among the world's most climate-vulnerable nations, and water scarcity from Himalayan glacier melt is already acute. The arms race displaces resources that both countries need for climate adaptation and social development, while nuclear-armed neighbors in escalation mode raises the structural risk level for the entire South Asian region.

Sub-Saharan Africa High Relevance
Debt Servicing Now Exceeds Health and Education Spending in 4 of 5 Governments
The IMF designates 22 low-income countries in Sub-Saharan Africa as in or at high risk of debt distress. The median country pays 8.8% interest on domestic debt. At the April 2026 Spring Meetings, the African Caucus demanded time-bound restructuring under the G20 Common Framework, calling for process deadlines and private creditor inclusion. IMF revised Sub-Saharan growth to 4.3% in 2026 — insufficient to meaningfully reduce debt burdens at current servicing rates.
Why It MattersCountries locked in debt servicing cycles cannot fund climate adaptation, education, or industrial policy for the just transition. This is the structural financing failure behind every "capacity gap" narrative in climate negotiations. Africa's demand for a rewrite of restructuring rules at the Spring Meetings signals that patience with technical incrementalism is exhausting. The political window for reform at MDBs and the IMF is narrowing.

Pacific Island States High Relevance
Pacific Resilience Facility Live; Pre-COP Secured — Sea Level Rise Now Irreversible for 30 Years
The Pacific Resilience Facility Agreement entered into force on May 6, 2026, establishing a dedicated regional climate finance mechanism to fill gaps in global instruments. Pacific leaders simultaneously secured a Pre-COP to be held in the Pacific region ahead of COP31 in Australia. NASA analysis confirms that Pacific Island nations including Tuvalu, Kiribati, and Fiji face at least 15cm of sea level rise in the next 30 years regardless of future emissions reductions — an existential planning horizon now certain.
Why It MattersThe Pacific is the moral and political frontline of climate diplomacy. Locking in a Pre-COP gives Pacific nations agenda-setting power before Australia hosts COP31, and the PRF represents the region building institutional infrastructure rather than waiting for global funds to be accessible. The NASA irreversibility finding hardens the case for adaptation finance as a justice obligation — not a development charity.

Philippines / SE Asia High Relevance
ASEAN Chair Philippines Pushes South China Sea Code of Conduct on 10th Anniversary of Arbitral Ruling
Vietnam and Philippines elevated bilateral ties at a June 1 summit, emphasizing freedom of navigation in the South China Sea. The Philippines as 2026 ASEAN Chair is pursuing Code of Conduct conclusion as a centerpiece — timed deliberately for the 10th anniversary of the 2016 South China Sea arbitral tribunal ruling that rejected China's expansive territorial claims. Indonesia, Philippines, and Vietnam are formalizing a maritime security cooperation triangle.
Why It MattersThe Philippines is attempting to lock in multilateral legal architecture for maritime disputes while it holds ASEAN chairmanship. The maritime security triangle signals a new willingness among Southeast Asian nations to build collective deterrence rather than manage bilateral relationships with Beijing separately. The outcome shapes how regional geopolitics intersects with climate-vulnerable coastal economies across the archipelago.

South Africa Medium Relevance
JETIP Pledges Hit $13.5B — South Africa's Just Transition Becoming a Model
International pledges for South Africa's Just Energy Transition Investment Plan have reached USD 13.5 billion, supporting renewable manufacturing, grid infrastructure, and skills development. Research from E3G contextualizes the JETIP against global geopolitical headwinds — rising US protectionism and EU carbon border mechanisms creating new pressures for the transition value chain. The Frontiers research journal published the first systematic assessment of poverty-reduction impacts from the plan.
Why It MattersSouth Africa's JETIP is the most closely watched large-economy just transition framework in the developing world. If it delivers on manufacturing jobs and community economic outcomes alongside decarbonization, it becomes the template other middle-income fossil fuel economies (Indonesia, India, Vietnam) can point to when negotiating similar deals. If it stalls, it validates skeptics who argue JETPs are finance mechanisms that serve donor country interests.

MENA Region High Relevance
MENA "Armed Peace" Fraying — Proxy Competition Replacing Interstate War
The US-brokered ceasefire following the June 2025 Israel-Iran 12-day war is holding technically but assessed as highly fragile. Analysts describe the truce as "incredibly weak" with Israeli strikes on Iran considered plausible in H1 2026. The post-war order is characterized by high-stakes proxy competition and resource-driven civil unrest. Saudi-Emirati competition has intensified in Sudan and southern Yemen following the STC's near-complete territorial consolidation. Syria's second year of post-Assad transition faces IS resurgence risk.
Why It MattersThe Strait of Hormuz closure during the 2025 conflict had cascading effects on global trade routes — Middle Corridor traffic surged, reshaping infrastructure investment priorities across Central Asia and the Caucasus. A return to armed conflict would re-trigger these shocks, with asymmetric impacts on Global South import-dependent economies. Energy price volatility from MENA instability also directly undercuts climate transition investment planning across the developing world.

Global Medium Relevance
Global South 2.0: 40% of World Output, Strategic Autonomy Doctrine Consolidating
The St. Petersburg International Economic Forum's June 2026 report declares a definitive transition from Western-led hegemony to "real multipolarity." Global South nations now represent 40% of global output and 44% of merchandise trade. The concept of "Global South 2.0" — more confident, pragmatic, and strategically autonomous — is being institutionalized through BRICS+ (now 11 members), G77, and bilateral South-South frameworks. Russia is accelerating reorientation toward the Global South as a strategic hedge against Western sanctions.
Why It MattersThis is the structural context for everything else in this scan. The consolidation of strategic autonomy discourse means that multilateral climate and finance negotiations will increasingly encounter Global South blocs that have alternatives to Western-conditioned finance and technology. For Diego's work at CECG and Hourglass, this consolidation changes the leverage calculus in UNFCCC, CBD, and IMF/World Bank reform conversations.

Emerging Strategic Patterns
01
Pattern — Minerals & Sovereignty
Minerals Sovereignty Is the New Climate Finance Battleground
The DRC-US deal is the clearest case, but the logic is spreading: great powers are treating minerals access as a new form of strategic currency, offering security guarantees, debt relief, and investment in exchange for upstream extraction rights. African nations are simultaneously being courted by the US, China, and EU with structurally similar offers. The question is whether "minerals diplomacy" can be reoriented toward domestic value-addition, industrialization, and community consent — or whether it reproduces extraction in new geopolitical clothing.

02
Pattern — Elections & Climate
2026 Election Cycles Are Reshaping Climate Governance — And Not for the Better
Brazil's October elections are already producing legislative regression on Amazon enforcement. The agribusiness caucus's timing is deliberate: pass the Destruction Package before Lula has enough political capital to veto it. The same dynamic is observable globally — wherever major elections fall in 2026, incumbent climate commitments face rollback pressure from extractive sector lobbies. Post-COP30 momentum is structurally vulnerable to electoral calendars in key emerging economies.
Brazil Colombia Indonesia
03
Pattern — Debt & Climate
The Debt-Climate Nexus Is Hardening Into a Structural Lock
Countries that cannot restructure debt cannot fund adaptation, industrial policy, or clean energy transitions. The Africa Spring Meetings push for G20 Common Framework reform, the UN warnings on development finance, and the climate change news analysis of debt-climate linkage all point to the same structural failure: the international financial architecture systematically prioritizes creditor returns over climate-vulnerable populations' survival. The political window for reform is narrowing as private creditors become dominant and resistant to restructuring.
Zambia Ghana Ethiopia Kenya Pakistan
04
Pattern — Armed Peace
"Armed Peace" Zones Are Multiplying — Each One a Climate and Development Liability
Three distinct "armed peace" situations are active simultaneously: the Israel-Iran ceasefire (fragile), the DRC-Rwanda-M23 ceasefire (fragmenting), and the India-Pakistan post-Sindoor standoff (frozen, not resolved). Each one consumes defense resources, displaces development investment, and creates geopolitical instability that undermines climate cooperation. The common thread is that US-brokered frameworks have produced agreements without institutional durability — and in at least two cases, have tied conflict resolution to economic arrangements that lack domestic legitimacy.
DRC Israel Iran India Pakistan
05
Pattern — Multipolarity
Strategic Autonomy Is No Longer a Slogan — It's Organizational Infrastructure
The Global South 2.0 framing emerging from the St. Petersburg Forum, BRICS+ expansion to 11 members, and the Pacific Resilience Facility's entry into force all reflect the same underlying dynamic: Global South actors are building institutional infrastructure — not just asserting political positions. The PRF creates regional climate finance with its own governance. BRICS agriculture diplomacy operationalizes food security cooperation. This organizational consolidation changes the leverage dynamics in every multilateral negotiation where Global South countries are asked to accept frameworks designed by others.
India (BRICS) Brazil Pacific Islands African Union
06
Pattern — Just Transition
Just Transition Is Being Tested as Real Policy — Results Will Define the Next Decade
South Africa's JETIP is the highest-stakes test of whether rich-country pledged finance for just transition actually delivers industrial transformation and community outcomes. If $13.5B in pledges translates into renewable manufacturing capacity, skills, and jobs — this becomes the model for Indonesia, India, Vietnam, and every other coal-dependent emerging economy. If it remains finance-paper that doesn't reach communities, it will poison the political well for future transition finance negotiations, giving ammunition to those who argue JETPs are instruments of northern economic interest.
