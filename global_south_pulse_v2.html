Top story of the moment — Score 5/5
The Strait of Hormuz is closed — and the Global South is paying the price

Since February 28, 2026, the US–Israel air campaign against Iran and the subsequent maritime blockade have disrupted 20% of global oil supply for 13 consecutive weeks. Operation Project Freedom was paused May 6, citing “great progress” toward an agreement. That agreement has not materialized.

The consequences for the Global South are severe and asymmetric. 84% of Gulf crude and 83% of LNG transiting the Strait was destined for Asia. Bangladesh and Pakistan are among the hardest-hit economies, facing fuel shortages, inflation spikes, and GDP contraction. India, as BRICS chair hosting the September summit, holds an impossible diplomatic brief: broker between strategic autonomy, energy dependency, and climate finance architecture simultaneously.

This is not a Middle East story — it is the central forcing function of Global South geopolitics in 2026, simultaneously active across every region in this scan.

DRC · Congo Apr 25
Mineral sovereignty offensive: cobalt quotas, audits, and 90 revoked mining titles
President Tshisekedi’s cabinet directive mandated a comprehensive audit of all mining exports targeting revenue leakage. CAMI revoked 90 titles covering 12,767 km² across five provinces. Cobalt export quotas for 2026–27 set at 96,600 MT annually — less than half 2024 output — creating ripple effects across Asian EV battery supply chains.
Why it matters

The most assertive mineral governance move by any African state in decades. It establishes a precedent: resource states can unilaterally restructure supply chains that Western and Chinese EV manufacturers had assumed were secured. If enforcement holds, it could template Zambia, Zimbabwe, and Namibia. If it collapses under capital flight, it will entrench the argument that sovereignty is performative without institutional capacity to back it.

Brazil May 25
Amazon at historic low — but 2026 elections put it back at risk
Deforestation in the Legal Amazon dropped to its lowest level since 2014, confirming a 50% reduction since Lula took office. The government committed R$3.1 billion ($617.5M) to the Eco Invest program targeting bioeconomy, sustainable tourism, and Amazon infrastructure. Lula’s same-day visit to Manaus was simultaneously ecological and electoral.
Why it matters

Brazil’s Amazon trajectory is one of the most consequential climate governance stories on the planet. The numbers are real and the trend is meaningful. But current polls show Lula in a tight race, and a more agriculture-friendly Congress is possible. The Eco Invest announcement signals that the Lula government understands Amazon success as a political asset to be actively managed, not merely a policy outcome. The risk is that this becomes campaign material before it becomes durable institutional architecture.

Bangladesh contributed negligibly to global emissions yet is absorbing consequences of decisions made in Washington, Tehran, and Tel Aviv.

This is the political argument for climate reparations and loss & damage in its most concrete form — a country that did not cause the problem, holds no leverage in the geopolitical dynamics producing it, and is paying the price.

Sub-Saharan Africa Mar 2026
Africa’s debt crisis tightens the climate finance trap
22 low-income African countries are in or at high risk of debt distress. Debt service has risen to 43% of budget revenue. Net capital flows to EMDEs have turned negative. The sovereign-bank nexus is growing fastest in Sub-Saharan Africa, where government debt problems can trigger banking crises.
Why it matters

Countries paying 43% of revenue on debt service cannot simultaneously invest in adaptation infrastructure, transition off fossil fuels, and maintain social spending. The irony is structural: Africa holds 30% of the world’s critical minerals — the raw material inputs for the very energy transition it cannot afford to finance for itself.

Global — Indigenous Peoples May 2026
Less than 1% of global climate finance reaches Indigenous peoples — UNPFII escalates
Between 2011 and 2020, Indigenous peoples and local communities received less than 1% of global climate finance despite managing at least 25% of carbon-capturing land globally. OHCHR opened a formal call for inputs on land demarcation and titling in 2026.
Why it matters

The 1% figure is not just morally indefensible — it is strategically irrational. Indigenous-managed territories consistently show better biodiversity and forest outcomes than state or corporate-managed equivalents. The OHCHR titling call for inputs may be the procedural step toward binding frameworks worth tracking for COP31 and the CBD.
